\section*{Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Summary}


This project examine the approach that use machine learning (ML) to predict hydrogen peroxide (H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2}) yield from optical emission spectroscopy (OES) in nanosecond pulsed CO\textsubscript{2} bubble discharge. The primary challenge is the high-dimensionality of OES data (701 wavelengths) relative to the small dataset (20 samples), which leads to overfitting and poor generalisation.

Four phases are conducted consecutively. First, baseline model are established among 7 different models based on PCA-selected features and discharge parameters, revealing that discharge parameters alone achieved R\textsuperscript{2} = 0.90 via Ridge regression while PCA of OES spectra failed to extract predictive information. Second, hyperparameter tuning via Optuna improved non-linear models but did not close the gap to the discharge-only baseline, confirming that the bottleneck was feature quality rather than model capacity. Then, 13 domain-knowledge-driven OES features were engineered as input of models, achieving substantial performance improvement across all models and closing the gap to baseline. Finally, feature importance analysis and backward elimination are conducted to identify features' contribution and improve model's generalisation ability. 

The results demonstrate that domain-knowledge-driven feature engineering, combined with principled dimensionality reduction, outperforms complex nonlinear models in small-sample high-dimensional regression tasks.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background and Motivation}

Plasma-based green chemistry has emerged as a promising approach for converting greenhouse gases into valuable chemicals. Nanosecond pulsed CO\textsubscript{2} bubble discharge enables efficient synthesis of hydrogen and hydrogen peroxide (H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2}) from CO\textsubscript{2} and water~\cite{Gao2024NSCO2Discharge, Shao2018PulsedDischarges}. As the approach advances toward industrial deployment, real-time monitoring is attached with more demand for close-loop production control. However, conventional H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} measurement relies on titration, an intrusive and time-consuming process.

Optical Emission Spectroscopy (OES) provides a non-intrusive observation method into the plasma by recording light emitted by excited species~\cite{Laux2003OESAir}. Traditional OES analysis employs spectroscopic techniques such as Boltzmann plots or line-ratio methods, which requires establishing physical model and expert interpretation~\cite{Laux2003OESAir, Srikar2024Accelerated}. Machine learning (ML) offers a data-driven alternative, enabling OES-based diagnostics for real-time applications~\cite{Gidon2019MLCAP}. However, high-dimensional complexity OES spectra is a challenge for ML models, for example, the OES used in this study comprises 701 wavelength points, creating a "dimensionality disaster", particularly on small datasets.

\subsection{Objectives}

This project addresses three objectives:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item Build ML/DL models to predict H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield from OES spectra and discharge parameters.
    \item Investigate whether domain-knowledge-driven feature engineering outperforms automated dimensionality reduction (PCA).
    \item Identify the minimal, physically interpretable feature set for a practical real-time predictor.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{State of the Art}

Recent studies have applied ML to automate OES-based plasma diagnostics. Srikar et al.~\cite{Srikar2025MLOES} combined PCA with random forest and deep neural networks for OES prediction in argon multi-jet plasma. Stefas et al.~\cite{Stefas2025MLSDBD} applied PCA with multilayer perceptrons to diagnose surface dielectric barrier discharges. Wang and Hsu~\cite{Wang2019MLOESAqueous} employed PCA followed by deep ANN to conduct plasma-in-liquid OES analysis, demonstrating that PCA alone was insufficient and deep network architectures were necessary. Park et al.~\cite{Park2021MLOESNitrogen} developed ML virtual metrology for electron density and temperature estimation from OES. A common thread across these studies is the reliance on PCA or partial least squares for dimensionality reduction of raw OES spectra prior to modelling.

PCA-based feature extraction, however, has fundamental limitations: it selects directions of maximum spectral variance, which need not correlate with the prediction target --- particularly on small datasets where PCA cannot discover task-relevant structure. Moreover, PCA discards the physical identity of individual emission lines, limiting interpretability and robustness to instrument drift. In contrast, Wang et al.~\cite{Wang2025MLOESCascaded} recently demonstrated that ML models trained on physically motivated line intensity ratios achieve $R^2 \approx 0.90$--$0.97$ for electron density and temperature prediction in cascaded arc plasma, validating the diagnostic power of domain-knowledge features. However, this approach has not been applied to chemical yield prediction.

Traditional spectroscopy has long recognised the value of line intensity ratios and band integrals for robust plasma diagnostics --- for example, nitrogen band ratios for electric field measurement~\cite{Paris2005N2Ratio} and OES techniques for atmospheric pressure plasmas~\cite{Laux2003OESAir}. This domain knowledge exists but is systematically overlooked in modern OES+ML pipelines. Meanwhile, ML studies in plasma catalysis have demonstrated that physically meaningful input features improve process prediction~\cite{Cai2024MLDRM, Wang2021PlasmaTarML}, and similar findings hold for biomass pyrolysis~\cite{Dong2023MLBiomass}. No prior work, however, has systematically compared PCA-based and domain-knowledge-based OES feature engineering for plasma product yield prediction. This project bridges that gap: it demonstrates that encoding traditional spectroscopic knowledge --- emission line intensities, band integrals, and spectroscopic ratios --- as ML features is the decisive factor for accurate OES-based H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield prediction, outperforming both automated dimensionality reduction and model complexity.

\section{Procedure / Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

The project aims to build experiment pipeline that predict  H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield from OES and/or discharge parameters using ML models including linear regression, MLP, RF and CNN. Four phases of experiments are conducted to examine a hypothesis each: (1)~whether PCA-based OES features carry enough predictive information, (2)~whether hyperparameter tuning release PCA features' representability, (3)~whether domain-knowledge-based features outperform PCA dimensional reduction, and (4)~which features are most important. The following subsections introduce the dataset, evaluation method, and work in each phase.


\subsection{Dataset and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:dataset}

The dataset was acquired from Gao et al.~\cite{Gao2024NSCO2Discharge}, who generously open sourced the data collected from nanosecond pulsed CO\textsubscript{2} bubble discharge experiments. There are 20~samples in the set,each comprising a 701-point OES spectrum, four discharge parameters (CO\textsubscript{2} flow rate, pulse frequency, pulse width, and rise time), and the H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield.

Leave-One-Out Cross Validation (LOOCV) is adopted as the evaluating method for model performance. It is appropriate especially in small data scale. Each of the 20~samples is picked once as test set, while the remaining 19 are used for training. LOOCV maximises training data per fold, ensures minimal random dataset split variability, and prevents data leakage. Primary performance metrics are coefficient of determination ($R^2$) and root mean square error (RMSE). 


\subsection{Input Configurations}

OES features and discharge parameters are combined in three different ways to test representation ability of each input category (Table~\ref{tab:configs}). Config~B (discharge parameters only) is set as a baseline that any OES-inclusive configuration must outperform Config~B to justify the additional complexity. Comparison between different configuration prevents a common pitfall in ML studies that adopting seemingly sophisticated method only to achieve the same result as the simplest way.

\subsection{Phase 3 --- Domain-Knowledge Feature Engineering}

The preceding phases relied on PCA for OES dimensionality reduction. However, PCA intrinsically selects directions of maximum variance in vector space, which need not have physical semantic meaning, especially when the dataset is small and the maximum variance direction does not align with \emph{decisive predictor}. Traditional plasma diagnostics has long used physically motivated spectral features for analysis. For example, line intensity ratios for electron temperature estimation~\cite{Laux2003OESAir}, band-head intensities for species identification~\cite{Paris2005N2Ratio},and spectroscopic ratios for electric field measurement. Wang et al.~\cite{Wang2025MLOESCascaded} recently validated this established approach for ML, achieving strong performance in predicting electron density. This project extends the principle to chemical yield prediction.

Driven by physical knowledge, 13 OES features were chosen to replace the 11 PCA components (Table~\ref{tab:oes_features}): seven emission line intensities, each corresponding to a species included in H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} formation pathways; three band integrals that capture broadband emission and are robust to spectral noise; and three spectroscopic ratios that encode relative species populations. Combined with the four discharge parameters, Config~C comprised 17~features in this phase.

These manually selected features brings higher interpretability than automated PCA. First, spectroscopic ratios are inherently drift-invariant: numerator and denominator are affected equally by instrument, eliminating system noise. Second, every feature maps to a known plasma species or reaction pathway, carrying physical meaning. Third, the reduction from 701 wavelengths to 13 features dramatically lowers dimensionality, reducing overfitting risk on this 20-sample dataset.

\subsection{Phase 4 --- Interpretability and Feature Reduction}

To achieve a consensus ranking on feature importance, model-specific methods are used to quantify: Ridge absolute standardised coefficients, PLS Variable Importance in Projection scores, RF permutation importance, and MLP SHAP values. The comprehensive ranking implicates non-biased feature importance across models.

Statistical validation includes two complementary tests. Bootstrap resampling (500 iterations) identifies 95\% confidence intervals for $R^2$ and RMSE, quantifying prediction uncertainty without distributional assumptions. Permutation test (2000 label shuffles) distinguishes if the mapping relationship learnt is genuine rather than overfitted by comparing observed $R^2$ with null-distribution results.

Feature reduction was performed with two strategies. Backward elimination iteratively removed the least important OES feature (retaining all four discharge parameters) to trace model generalization ability with less features. Category ablation removed entire OES feature types --- emission lines, band integrals, or spectroscopic ratios --- one at a time to identify which category contributes most. Together, these analyses identify most informative features and is expected to improve model generalization ability.

\section{Findings / Results}
\label{sec:results}

This section reports the outcome of the four phases mentioned in \S\ref{sec:method}. Phases~1 and~2 test the ability of PCA-projected OES features and hyperparameter tuning; result reject the hypothesis that these techniques are sufficient. Phase~3 then tests whether physically motivated OES features reveal the predictive signal, and Phase~4 removes redundancy and identifies the minimal subset of features needed. The primary finding is the considerable shift between Phases~1--2 and Phase~3, together with its statistical confirmation on the pruned model in Phase~4.

\subsection{Phase 1 \& 2 --- Baseline Ceiling and the PCA Dead End}

The four discharge parameters alone already act as strong predictive indicator in H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield: Ridge Config~B achieved R\textsuperscript{2}~$=0.904$ and PLS Config~B achieved R\textsuperscript{2}~$=0.898$ (Table~\ref{tab:phase_comparison}). This sets a strong reference ceiling --- any OES-inclusive configuration must exceed Config~B to justify the additional processing cost. Figure~\ref{fig:phase1_bar} shows the full 7-model $\times$ 3-config grid: Config~B dominates with linear models, while Config~C trails Config~B across every model class. XGBoost produced an identical R\textsuperscript{2}~$=-0.108$ across all three configurations, a default-hyperparameter artefact unsuitable for $n=20$ under LOOCV, and is excluded from subsequent analysis.

Under PCA, adding OES to the discharge parameters does not help --- it \emph{hurts}. Ridge Config~C fell to R\textsuperscript{2}~$=-0.175$ and PLS Config~C to R\textsuperscript{2}~$=0.625$. The failure is structural rather than an artifact of improper dimensional reduction: the 11 principal components retained already preserve $\geq\!95\%$ of the spectral variance (Fig.~\ref{fig:pca_variance}), so no meaningful amount of variance was discarded. The instead interpretation is that maximum-variance directions in the OES spectrum need not align with the H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} target, especially at $n=20$, and the PCA components that dominate the reduced representation introduce noise that linear models cannot ignore.

Tuned MLP Config~B reached $0.861$, approaching but not matching the linear baseline. This rejects the second hypothesis: tuning closes part of the gap, but the ceiling on OES-inclusive models is set by feature quality, not by model capacity. Features engineering themselves is the lever for further improvement.

\subsection{Phase 3 --- The Domain-Knowledge Step-Change}

11 PCA components are replaced by the 13 physically motivated OES features described in \S\ref{sec:method}. Result shows that Ridge Config~C rose from $-0.175$ to $\mathbf{0.798}$, a improvement of $\Delta$R\textsuperscript{2}~$\approx+0.97$; MLP Config~C rose from $-1.131$ to $\mathbf{0.815}$, a swing of $\Delta$R\textsuperscript{2}~$\approx+1.95$; PLS Config~C rose from $0.625$ to $0.744$. Table~\ref{tab:phase_comparison} and Figure~\ref{fig:phase_comparison} show that the Config~B converges significantly to Config~C. Re-encoding OES spectra with physical knowledge, unlocks the predictive potential that PCA failed to capture. Two observations reinforce the interpretation. First, the improvement is not confined to a specific model family: linear, kernel, tree-based, and deep models all benefit, indicating that the bottleneck was the feature representation itself. Second, Ridge --- the simplest of the seven models --- now matches the tuned neural networks on Config~C, suggesting that the feature--target relationship in this reaction is essentially linear. This implies the project's central finding: appropriate feature engineering, instead of model complexity or hyperparameter tuning, is the decisive factor for OES-based H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield prediction.

\subsection{Phase 4 --- Interpretability, Uncertainty, and the Minimal Model}

With the domain features established, Phase~4 explored which features dominate the prediction, how confident the result is, and whether the 13-feature set can be reduced. The consensus feature ranking is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:feature_importance} and visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:importance_heatmap}. CO\textsubscript{2} flow rate is the most influential predictor (mean rank 1.75), followed by the CO\textsubscript{2}\textsuperscript{+} band integral (4.75) and pulse width (5.25). Bootstrap resampling (500 iterations) quantifies how tight these point estimates actually are (Table~\ref{tab:bootstrap}, Figure~\ref{fig:bootstrap_dist}). At $n=20$, the 95\% confidence intervals are wide and overlap on some portion. Ridge Config~B [0.800, 0.955] and Ridge Config~C [0.574, 0.910] overlap on large portion, which means the 13 domain-knowledge OES Config~C is not \emph{statistically} separable from the Config~B baseline. The Ridge Config~C and MLP Config~C intervals ([0.647, 0.883]) also overlap, indicating that the non-linear model has limited advantage over the linear model at this feature set (linear plasma chemistry reaction). These overlaps are not a weakness of the approach but a result of the small dataset scale. It thus motivates the following feature reduction: if the signal lives in a small subset of the 13 features, pruning should tighten the estimate.

Two independent reduction strategies answer the redundancy question and agree with each other. Taking Ridge as an example, the category ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, upper block) removes different OES feature types while keeping discharge parameters: removing everything except the three spectroscopic ratios yields R\textsuperscript{2}~$=0.906$, and removing everything except the three band integrals yields R\textsuperscript{2}~$=0.905$. Both match the discharge-only Config~B ($0.904$) and substantially exceed the full 13-feature set ($0.798$). Single-wavelength intensities, in contrast, deliver the weakest performance ($0.823$), consistent with their sensitivity to absolute intensity drift. The backward elimination trajectory (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, lower block) implies the same story: R\textsuperscript{2} rises from $0.798$ at 13 OES features to a peak of $0.918$ at one OES feature (the CO\textsubscript{2}\textsuperscript{+} band integral), then drops back to $0.904$ when no OES features is included, confirming that OES adds more information besides discharge parameters, but only through a small number of non-redundant channels. The peak models from each strategy are close but not identical: backward elimination favours a 5-feature model (1 OES band + 4 discharge, R\textsuperscript{2}~$=0.918$), while category ablation preserves all three ratios (3 ratios + 4 discharge, 7 features). After refitting Ridge model on the 7-feature set, R\textsuperscript{2} is raised from $0.906$ (category ablation result) to $\mathbf{0.920}$, and a 2000-permutation label-shuffle test on that refit model returned no null R\textsuperscript{2} as large as the observed value, excludes the possibility of overfitting. The 7-feature model is the recommended configuration because it retains all three ratio types --- OH/H\textsubscript{$\alpha$}, O/OH, and H\textsubscript{$\alpha$}/H\textsubscript{$\beta$} --- which are inherently drift-invariant and physically interpretable, whereas the simpler model suggested by backward elimination depends on a single band integral.

In summary, the evidences are chained mutually. Phases~1--2 reject the hypotheses that PCA-projected OES features and hyperparameter tuning alone can predict H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield. Phase~3 demonstrates that physically motivated OES features produce a step-change across every model class, closing the Config~B / Config~C gap. Phase~4 then shows that most of the 13 domain features are redundant, and that a 7-feature Ridge model built on three drift-invariant spectroscopic ratios and the four discharge parameters achieves R\textsuperscript{2}~$=0.920$ with statistically significant permutation support. Though the gap between ridge and neural network is negligible --- the bootstrap intervals overlap ---, by Occam's razor, Ridge model in this project ($n=20$) is more preferable dut to complexity and interpretability advantages.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

The three objectives posed in Section~\ref{sec:intro} have each been resolved. The first, to build machine-learning models predicting H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2} yield from OES and discharge parameters, is met by a pruned seven-feature Ridge regression attaining $R^2 = 0.920$ and $\mathrm{RMSE} = 0.066$ under LOOCV. The second, to test whether domain-knowledge features outperform automated dimensionality reduction, is met by the Phase~1 to Phase~3 step-change on Config~C, in which Ridge rises from $R^2 = -0.175$ to $0.798$ and MLP from $-1.131$ to $0.815$. The third, to identify a minimal interpretable feature set, is met by the convergence of backward elimination and category ablation on a model of at most seven features.

The decisive lever was domain-knowledge feature engineering, not model capacity or hyperparameter tuning. Phase~1 rejected the hypothesis that PCA-projected OES features carry sufficient signal: adding eleven PCA components to the four discharge parameters collapsed Ridge Config~C from the Config~B baseline of $R^2 = 0.904$ down to $-0.175$. Phase~2 then rejected the hypothesis that tuning could rescue those features' representability, with the best tuned OES-included result (CNN Config~C) climbing only from $0.688$ to $0.775$, still below Ridge Config~B. Only when the PCA components were replaced with thirteen physically motivated OES features did the Config~B / Config~C gap close across every model class.

The reproducible artefact recommended by this research is a Ridge regression on three drift-invariant spectroscopic ratios (OH/H\textsubscript{$\alpha$}, O/OH, H\textsubscript{$\alpha$}/H\textsubscript{$\beta$}) together with the four discharge parameters. On this seven-feature set the observed $R^2 = 0.920$ is statistically significant under a permutation test of 2000 label shuffles, in which no null realisation matched or exceeded the observed value ($p < 0.0005$). The preference for Ridge over a non-linear alternative is dictated not by a capability gap but by complexity and deployment cost at small scale data $n=20$.

However, several limitations must be stated plainly. At the thirteen-feature stage, the bootstrap intervals of Ridge Config~B $[0.800, 0.955]$ and Ridge Config~C $[0.574, 0.910]$ overlap substantially, so a claim that domain features are statistically superior to PCA is \emph{not} defensible before pruning; the permutation significance attaches only to the pruned seven-feature model. The sample size of twenty is the root cause of these wide intervals and of the project's inability to discriminate between linear and non-linear model families. All data originate from a single reactor and a single wavelength calibration, so cross-reactor generalisation remains untested, and the thirteen OES features themselves were derived for CO\textsubscript{2}-water bubble chemistry and will need to be re-derived for other plasma systems.

The broader implication returns to the gap identified in Section~\ref{sec:intro}: modern OES-based machine-learning pipelines default to PCA or PLS and overlook the diagnostic domain knowledge that traditional spectroscopy has encoded for decades, i.e. line ratios, band integrals, drift-invariant markers. On small, physically structured datasets those overlooked knowledge proves to be the dominant predictive lever, and the pruned Ridge $R^2 \approx 0.92$ sits within the $R^2 \approx 0.90\text{--}0.97$ band reported by Wang et al.~\cite{Wang2025MLOESCascaded} for line-intensity-ratio models on a different plasma-diagnostic task. The questions of cross-reactor generalisation and live deployment that these limitations raise are taken up in Section~\ref{sec:future}.

\section{Recommendations / Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Generalisation testing:} Apply the domain-knowledge feature engineering framework to OES datasets from different plasma reactor types (e.g., DBD, microwave) to assess transferability of the selected emission lines and ratios.

    \item \textbf{Real-time deployment:} Integrate the 7-feature Ridge model with live OES hardware to enable closed-loop discharge control. Evaluate prediction latency, calibration stability, and robustness to instrument drift.

    \item \textbf{Extended datasets:} Collect additional samples across wider operating parameter ranges to investigate whether non-linear models (MLP, RF) offer advantages over linear models at larger data scales.

    \item \textbf{Physics-informed modelling:} For multi-product plasma systems, incorporate conservation constraints (e.g., mass balance) into multi-output regression models to improve physical consistency.

    \item \textbf{Transfer learning:} Leverage models trained on this dataset to initialise predictors for related plasma reactions (ozone generation, N\textsubscript{2}O conversion), reducing the data requirements for new systems.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reflection}
\label{sec:reflection}


Throughout this project, I have experienced the full cycle of academic research: from conducting a literature review, to processing data, building, testing, and evaluating models, and finally articulating the findings in the form of an academic research paper.

A considerable portion of my time was spent on looking up suitable data and literature reading. This experience that offered a candid glimpse into the practical challenges of "scientific research". It gives me a comprehensive perspective on what a future research career might be like.

From a methodological standpoint, the project was structured as a chain of evidence, which serves as a training for logical and rigorous thinking. By progressing through successive experimental phases and analysing each set of results in turn, I was able to produce solid, incremental findings through careful control of variables.

On the technical side, the project taught me the importance of exercising discretion when adopting seemingly sophisticated techniques such as automated PCA dimensionality reduction. In this work, well-established, domain-informed feature selection outperformed the automated approach by a substantial margin, providing a valuable reminder to remain critical and evidence-driven when choosing methods in future research.
